\textbf{Abstract}\\
The abstract should be at most one page in length, giving a very high-level overview of your project. Your abstract should state the following:
\begin{itemize}
    \item What your project is
    \item Why you chose it / what are your goals
    \item What your project does / what problem does it address
    \item How you did it / what did you use
    \item What does it accomplish / what goals were achieved
\end{itemize}

This guide is designed to assist you in writing your project documentation. Different chapters contain instructions regarding all aspects of you document and project. In addition. This document is formatted as a template, and can be used as a starting point for your documentation. All of the font sizes, margins, etc are correct, and the chapter layout presented is an example of what is expected, but will need to be modified to suit your specific project. You should consult you supervisor regarding this. General and overall project/documentation instructions and comments are found in most chapters below, marked with the tag \textbf{``General Material''}. Each chapter also contains chapter-specific instructions specific to that chapter. These instructions are marked with the tag \textbf{``Chapter Specific Material''}.

It is advised that you look at past project documentation in the library, however bear in mind that guidelines and regulations change over time. Thus you should not use past documentation as anything but to get a general feel of what project documentation looks and feels like. This guide is up to date and supersedes any formatting or parameters of past documentation.

\chapter{Introduction}

\section{General Material}

First and foremost you must understand what your project is. \textbf{It is a major contribution to your degree}. Your project must address several important points, including your ability to:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Understand the state-of-the-art of a specific area: You will need to learn, know and adapt to the most current technologies in your area.
    \item Learn and apply new technologies on your own: The ability to work under your own inertia on new/changing technologies is important.
    \item Develop and demonstrate a productive relationship with your supervisor: Collaboration and working productively with others is a very important aspect of your abilities. Co-workers, colleagues, and bosses will always be there.
    \item Demonstrate a comprehensive understanding of the development lifecycle: You need to know what a project entails, top to bottom, start to finish.
\end{itemize}

Your work should reflect on your ability to:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Take an idea, do background research, understand it thoroughly, and develop and demonstrate an application based on that idea.
    \item Describe your project in a well structured and formatted documentation so that others can understand what you have achieved.
    \item Demonstrate a working application, simulation results, etc.
    \item Discuss your project with faculty examiners, answering any questions put to you regarding your project.
    \item Take something you have learned and push it further - to take it beyond the classroom.
\end{itemize}

Your Project should be critical and analytical. Your project and especially your documentation should be focused on not just ``doing'', but explaining. Why are you doing this? What does it do? Why are you doing it this way? Why is it different? Why is it better? Why is it worse? Where are its strengths? Where are its weaknesses? Where is it going? Where is it not going? Notice that the word ``how'' has not yet been mentioned. How you complete your project is only one part of the whole. Equally important to the ``hows'' are these ``whys'' - the decisions you made along the way and the results and impacts of those decisions.

The title page, as in this templated, is centered and not numbered. All other front matter (Disclaimer, Table of Contents, Acknowledgements, Abstracts, etc.) each start on their own page (except for lists of equations/figures/tables if they ares short) and are numbered in lower case Roman numerals (i, ii, iii, iv, v, vi, ...). The Table of Contents should be automatically generated by LaTeX. However the list of equations/figures/tables while automatic will require a little bit of help from you in that you must mark and reference all of your equations/figures/tables for this generation. Do not forget to sign and date the Disclaimer of your final bound copy which you will turn into the faculty.

Starting on the first page of Chapter 1, page numbering should start over (from 1) and count normally (1, 2, 3, etc.). Font should be 12pt, fully justified, with 1.5 line spacing with standard LaTeX margins (all set in this document). Consistency is key to your documentation. All headings should have the same style, all margins should be the same, etc.

The exact chapter layout of your documentation should be discussed with your supervisor and made to fit your project. A traditional layout would be the following:

\section{General Material}

References are a very important part of your work and must be done carefully and correctly. References are how readers of your work will connect and relate your work to the work of others and your topic area in general. Without proper referencing your documentation would only be a description of work, not a piece of work on its own, that is related to your field.

\subsection{Bibliography Style:}

\begin{itemize}
    \item The Bibliography (or References) must contain a list of books, journals, and conference articles and all other material cited in the main body of text (except websites).
    \item Entries in the bibliography must contain: author(s), title, 
conference / journal, publisher, date of publication, and possibly other 
reference-specific information. Consult your supervisor.
    \item Each entry in the bibliography is numbered consecutively in order of appearance, such as [1], [2], etc. These citation numbers are included in the main body of the text in square brackets.
    \item All bibliographical information is exclusively included in the list of ``References'' section at the end of the document, next to the respective citation number.
    \item Please see the following example.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Bibliography Example:}

\textbf{Example body of text below:}

``A prefix labelling technique presented in \cite{prefixlabel} seems to be appropriate for topologies similar to a Tree structure. Our research focuses on developing a prefix labelling technique of the B-Tree topology \cite{btree}. Once a network is organised as a B-Tree the prefix can be calculated using a distributed process as suggested in \cite{prefixlabel}. The ultimate aim is to achieve load balancing for distributed systems \cite{loaddistrib}, \cite{loadparallel} organised as a B-Tree topology''

See Bibliography for the references.

\textbf{End of example}

\subsection{Web References}

All referenced websites must be \textit{terminal references}. A terminal 
reference is the ``root'' reference for a specific idea 
/ hypothesis / theory / concept / produce / technology / etc. For instance, 
\textit{no} Wikipedia pages are terminal references. All Wikipedia pages have 
references to other (possibly terminal) references! Another way to view a 
terminal references is as the reference where the ``chain of reference'' stops. 
A chain of reference is (for example) when someone discussing \textit{concept A} 
refers to a website, which refers to a Wikipedia site on concept A, which refers 
to another website, which refers to a book, which refers to another book, which 
then refers to a a scholarly journal article, which presented concept A for the 
first authoritative time. That journal article is the terminal reference for 
concept A. In some cases, websites may be terminal references, however, this is 
rare. Another time that a website may be referenced is when the website is the 
official website or portal to a specific tool/technology/etc.

Websites \textit{are not} included in the bibliography/references section. They are included as footnotes.

\textbf{Example}

``Although the number of proteins with known structure continues to grow, the number of proteins with known sequence, but unknown structure is growing faster. Thus the gap between the number of proteins of known structure and the number of proteins of known sequence is growing. As protein structure dictates protein function, and proteins of similar sequences often have similar structure and therefore function, databases of protein sequence and structure information such as UniProt\footnote{\label{uniprot}http://www.uniprot.org} have become increasingly useful to both those who are sequencing proteins, and those that are predicting protein structure.''

\textbf{Note the footnote at the bottom of the page, corresponding to the \ref{uniprot} above. This is how websites should be cited.}

\textbf{End of Example}

All references other than websites should be added in LaTeX as an entry in a BibTeX file. BibTeX will then give you options on how to present your references, and automatically generate your bibliography.

This chapter \textit{begins} to explain how you accomplished your project and your objectives. Explain here what was needed to implement your project, both in technology and effort. \section{General Material:}

A note on screenshots: Screenshots should be used carefully and sparingly. Only 
use those that are very explainatory in nature. A good rule of thumb is that if 
it would take many lines of text (with a footprint larger than that of hte 
screenshot, caption, and brief text explaination combined) to explain what you 
want, use the screenshot/caption/brief text explanation. Otherwise just 
describe in text.

Tables, figures, and equations may appear throughout your documentation. All 
tables and figures should be centred and have captions. Captions should be 
inserted in MS Word with ``Caption...'' after selecting or right-clicking the 
table/figure. In LaTeX captions are provided as part of the includegraphics 
command. This will allow you to generate a list of tables and/or figures for 
your front matter, similar to your table of contents. For an example see Figure 
5.1

\begin{figure}
    \begin{center}
    \includegraphics{logo.png}
    \caption{sample figure}
    \end{center}
\end{figure}

Equations may also appear through your documentation. If you refer back to a 
particular equation in your documentation more than once, you should centre and 
number the equation, allowing you to refer to it by number, and to allow you to 
generate a list of equations, similar to your lists of tables and figures.

\textbf{Example:}

Einstien's famous mass-energy equivalence is given in Equation (5.1)

\begin{equation}
    E = mc^{2}
\end{equation}

Where \textit{E} is energy, \textit{m} is mass, and \textit{c} is the speed of 
light. Equation (5.1) can be rearranged to an expression for the speed of light 
as in Equation (5.2)

\begin{equation}
    c = \sqrt{E / m}
\end{equation}

\textbf{End of Example}

Equations should be inserted with the equation environment of LaTeX and it will 
handle the rest.
